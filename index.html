Breast Health Summary

The basics you need to know - To be breast aware, know your B.R.A.S.

B

Breast aware

Be breast aware from 20 and know what's normal for you. Check your breasts regularly.

  • Know what is normal for your breasts
  • Check your breasts regularly for changes
  • Report changes immediately to your doctor
R

Reduce your risk through healthy lifestyle choices. Know your family history and talk to your doctor about hereditary breast cancer concerns.

  • Maintain a healthy lifestyle
  • Know your family history
  • Discuss hereditary concerns with your doctor
A

Act promptly

Act on unusual breast changes, show your doctor promptly.

  • Don't wait - act on any unusual changes
  • Show your doctor any concerns immediately
  • Early detection saves lives
S

Screening mammograms

Consider having screening mammograms annually from 40.

  • Start annual mammograms at age 40
  • Continue regular screening
  • Discuss timing with your doctor

Remember...

  • Most lumps and bumps are not cancerous, but show your doctor if you notice any breast changes.
  • Your risk of breast cancer increases as you get older.
  • Regular mammograms from 40 can help find breast cancer early when it's more treatable.

How to be breast aware

Breast cancer is the most common cancer in women. Your risk increases as you get older, so every woman needs to be breast aware from the age of 20.

Mammogram Recommendation

Annual mammograms generally start from age 40. Discuss with your doctor about the best screening schedule for you.

Find it before you feel it - Mammograms detect breast cancer early

Consider an annual screening mammogram from the age of 40

A mammogram is a safe, low-dose x-ray that can pick up very small breast cancers, long before a lump can be felt.

92% Survival Rate

Women have a 92% chance of surviving breast cancer 10 years or longer if the cancer is detected by a mammogram.

Checking your breasts regularly is important

Follow these three simple steps to check your breasts regularly

Touch

Touch both breasts Press quite firmly using three fingers. You're feeling for any lumps or thickening in the breast, even up to the collarbone and into the armpits.

Look

Look in a mirror Raise your arms above your head. Does this change the appearance of your breasts or nipples?

Check

Check with your doctor Check any breast changes with your doctor, even if you've had a mammogram recently

Risks you can't change

We don't know exactly what causes breast cancer, but we do know that certain risk factors can increase your chance of getting it

Being a woman

This is the highest risk factor for developing breast cancer.

Getting older

Age is the second most important risk factor.

Family history

Although most people with breast cancer have no family history, you may have a higher risk of developing breast cancer if your mother, sister or daughter has had breast and/or ovarian cancer.

Important

Discuss the incidence of breast cancer in your family and talk to your doctor if a family history exists.
